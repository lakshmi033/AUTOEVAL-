ANSWER KEY  
Q1. History is the study of past events, especially those related to human life and societies. It helps us understand how people lived, what they achieved, and how societies developed over time. History provides information about important events, cultures, and civilizations of the past. Studying history is important because it helps us learn from past experiences and understand the present better. It also helps us develop a sense of identity, culture, and national heritage.

History, study of past events, human societies, development of societies, past cultures and civilizations, understanding the past, learning from past experiences, understanding the present, national heritage, identity and culture.

During this time, the British made laws, controlled trade, and influenced political and social life. Colonial rule led to economic exploitation but also created strong movements for independence.

Q6. The Revolt of 1857 was the first major uprising against British rule in India. It was caused by several political, economic, social, and military factors that created widespread dissatisfaction among Indians.  

One of the main political causes was the British policy of expansion. The British used policies like the Doctrine of Lapse, through which they annexed many Indian kingdoms if the ruler did not have a natural heir. This caused anger among Indian rulers and princes who lost their power and territories. Another important cause was economic exploitation. The British imposed heavy taxes on farmers and destroyed traditional industries such as handicrafts. Many artisans and workers lost their livelihoods, and the economic condition of the people became very poor. There were also social and religious causes. Many Indians believed that the British were interfering with their religious and social customs. The introduction of new laws and reforms created fear among people that their traditions and religions were under threat.  

A major military cause was the introduction of the Enfield rifle cartridges, which were rumored to be greased with the fat of cows and pigs. Since cows are sacred to Hindus and pigs are forbidden in Islam, this deeply hurt the religious feelings of Indian soldiers. In addition, Indian soldiers in the British army were treated unfairly and paid less compared to British soldiers.  

All these political, economic, social, and military causes together led to widespread dissatisfaction among the people and finally resulted in the Revolt of 1857, which became an important event in India’s struggle for independence.  

KEY WORDS  
Revolt of 1857, first war of independence, uprising against British rule, political causes, Doctrine of Lapse, annexation of kingdoms, economic exploitation, heavy taxation, decline of handicrafts, poverty of farmers and artisans, social and religious interference, fear of losing traditions, Enfield rifle cartridges, cow and pig fat controversy, dissatisfaction among Indian soldiers, unfair treatment in the army, causes of the revolt.  

Q7. The Indus Valley Civilization was one of the earliest and most advanced ancient civilizations in the world. It developed around the Indus River and its surrounding regions around 2500 BCE. The main cities of this civilization were Harappa and Mohenjo-daro, which show evidence of a highly developed urban culture.  

One of the most important features of the Indus Valley Civilization was its well-planned cities. The streets were laid out in a grid pattern, and houses were constructed using baked bricks. Many houses had multiple rooms and courtyards, showing advanced architectural planning.

Another remarkable feature was the advanced drainage system. Almost every house was connected to a well-organized drainage system with covered drains along the streets. This shows that the people had a good understanding of sanitation and cleanliness. The civilization also had a strong economic system. People were mainly engaged in agriculture, growing crops such as wheat and barley. They also practiced trade and exchanged goods with other regions. The use of seals, weights, and measures indicates the presence of organized trade and administration. The Indus Valley people were also skilled in crafts and technology. They made pottery, jewelry, tools, and ornaments from materials such as bronze, copper, and gold.

Thus, the Indus Valley Civilization was known for its advanced urban planning, drainage system, agriculture, trade, and skilled craftsmanship, making it one of the most important civilizations of the ancient world.

KEY WORDS
Indus Valley Civilization, ancient urban civilization, Indus River region, 2500 BCE, Harappa, Mohenjo-daro, planned cities, grid pattern streets, baked brick houses, drainage system, sanitation, agriculture, wheat and barley cultivation, trade and commerce, seals, weights and measures, crafts and technology, pottery, jewelry making, bronze and copper tools, organized society.

Q8. Mahatma Gandhi played a crucial role in the Indian National Movement and became one of the most important leaders in India’s struggle for independence. He believed strongly in the principles of truth (Satya) and non-violence (Ahimsa) and used these methods to oppose British rule in India. Gandhi believed that peaceful resistance and civil disobedience could bring justice and freedom. One of his major contributions was leading the Non-Cooperation Movement in 1920, where Indians were encouraged to boycott British goods, government schools, and institutions. This movement increased national awareness and united people against British rule. Another important movement led by Gandhi was the Civil Disobedience Movement in 1930, which began with the famous Salt March to Dandi. Gandhi protested against the British salt tax and encouraged people to break unjust laws peacefully.

In 1942, Gandhi launched the Quit India Movement, demanding that the British leave India immediately. This movement created strong national unity and intensified the demand for independence.

Through these movements, Gandhi mobilized millions of people including farmers, workers, and students. His leadership promoted unity, self-reliance, and national pride. Because of his dedication to India’s freedom and his peaceful methods, he became one of the most respected leaders of the Indian National Movement.

KEY WORDS
Mahatma Gandhi, Indian National Movement, freedom struggle, principles of truth and non-violence, Satya, Ahimsa, Non-Cooperation Movement 1920, boycott of British goods, Civil Disobedience Movement 1930, Salt March, Dandi March, protest against salt tax, Quit India Movement 1942, demand for British to leave India, mass participation, national unity, peaceful resistance, struggle for independence.

Q9. The First World War was a major global conflict that lasted from 1914 to 1918. It involved many powerful nations of Europe and later spread to other parts of the world. The war was mainly caused by political tensions, rivalry among nations, and complex alliances between countries. One important cause of the war was militarism. Many European countries began to build strong armies and weapons to increase their military power. This created fear and tension among nations. Another cause was the system of alliances. Countries formed military groups to protect themselves. The two main groups were the Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Italy) and the Triple Entente (Britain, France, and Russia). These alliances increased the chances of a large-scale war. Imperialism was another important cause. European countries competed with each other to control colonies and territories around the world. This rivalry created conflicts and hostility among nations. Nationalism also played a role, as many people strongly supported their own countries and wanted greater power and independence. The immediate cause of the war was the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary in 1914 by a Serbian nationalist. This event triggered a chain reaction of declarations of war among the allied countries.

The First World War had many serious effects. It resulted in the death of millions of soldiers and civilians and caused massive destruction of cities, industries, and infrastructure. Several powerful empires such as the German Empire, Austro-Hungarian Empire, Ottoman Empire, and Russian Empire collapsed after the war.

The war ended with the signing of the Treaty of Versailles in 1919, which placed heavy penalties on Germany. The war also led to major political and social changes, including the formation of new nations and changes in international relations.

Thus, the First World War had a lasting impact on world history and changed the political map of many countries.

KEY WORDS
First World War, 1914–1918, global conflict, militarism, alliances, Triple Alliance, Triple Entente, imperialism, competition for colonies, nationalism, assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand, outbreak of war, death of millions, destruction of cities, collapse of empires, German Empire, Austro-Hungarian Empire, Ottoman Empire, Russian Empire, Treaty of Versailles 1919, political changes, new nations.

Q10. India’s independence in 1947 was the result of a long and continuous struggle against British colonial rule. Many important movements, leaders, and events played a significant role in strengthening the demand for freedom.

One of the major events was the Non-Cooperation Movement in 1920, led by Mahatma Gandhi. In this movement, Indians were asked to boycott British goods, schools, courts, and government institutions. This movement encouraged people to oppose British rule through peaceful methods and increased national awareness.

Another important event was the Civil Disobedience Movement in 1930. Gandhi began this movement with the famous Salt March to Dandi to protest against the British salt tax. Thousands of people joined the movement and broke unjust laws peacefully.

The Quit India Movement in 1942 was another significant event. During this movement, Gandhi called for the British to leave India immediately. The movement spread across the country and showed the strong determination of Indians to achieve independence.

After the Second World War, Britain faced economic and political difficulties and could no longer maintain control over India. As a result, the British government decided to grant independence. Finally, India achieved independence on 15 August 1947, ending nearly two centuries of British rule.

KEY WORDS
India’s independence 1947, Indian freedom struggle, British colonial rule, Mahatma Gandhi, Non-Cooperation Movement 1920, boycott of British goods and institutions, Civil Disobedience Movement 1930, Salt March, Dandi March, protest against salt tax, Quit India Movement 1942, demand for British to leave India, Second World War, end of British rule, independence on 15 August 1947.
